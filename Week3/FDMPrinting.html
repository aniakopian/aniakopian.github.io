Week 3 - FDM Printing

Creating a Plant Holder

This week we were assigned to create a plant holder that fits in our desk organizer created last week! Here is the prucaslicer! otherwise known as one of the kinds of FDM printers. FDM printers are machines that extrude plastic filaments by melting them into the programmed form. There are a few different kinds of filaments, in our model we will be using PLA. When creating the models, the printers start from the base of your model and move its way up.

This is the prucaslicer in action. This image was taken during my FDM training where we learned to create a keychain! The keychain as you can see is starting from the very base before being built up.

Right here, you can see a curvy pattern being extruded through the nozzle. The style of this can change depending on how solid or how hollow you wish your piece to be, for our design, this is how our keychain was programmed to be solidified inside.

The plant holder I created, started right from these few circles! To create the 3D model of this plant holder we used the help of Fusion360, just like last week. In this image you can see the circle sketches for the base, as well as the ones that were then joined together above to create the base shape of the pot.

Here is the basic shape of my pot once the base was extruded and each circle was lofted to the base! The next step is to create a pattern.

This is my final model. I added a sketch of a thin rectangle to the exterior of my pot and then extruded it. While extruding, I had to make sure it was connecting to the pot but still saving it as a new body. I then filleted the edges to be more round and shape to the pot. Once I was happy with the shape, I was ready to create a circular pattern. I had my pattern repeat 50 times around the pot, which created the image to the left!

For our printer, I downloaded the software PrusaSlicer. When exporting my file from Fusion360, I exported it as an STL which is a format typically used for 3D printing. This format can be accepted by Prusa to then give me details on how long my print will take, how my print is sliced from start to finish, etc.

When I imported my file, I immediately went to slice the model to see if everything looked right. This image shows the base rim of my pot which is slightly raised to fit inside the pre-existing slot cut out in the desk organizer. This rim goes up about 0.5" before the actual base is created.

Here you can see the base I mentioned and how the printer extruded filament to make it secure. Prusa's estimated print time is not always correct. Here it says around 10 hours but in reality mine took about 13 and this is at 0.20mm speed. 3D printing takes time!

This is the full model of my pot in Prusa!

Once I was happy with my plan of action, I exported this file to the SD cards that go into the 3D printers. Once you insert the card into the machine you can see your file and select it on the printer. I started by using the green PLA filament which was already attached to the nozzle. After about ten minutes, I noticed the filament was all tangled and I couldn't go on unless I extruded it.

I took this opportunity to take out this filament and switch it to a different one because I was feeling picky that day I guess. I swapped it out to his pink color and after about 45 minutes, you can see the base starting to form.

Earlier I was showing this base on PrusaSlicer and you can see it printed out exactly as it showed it would!

With about 9 more hours to go this is where my model stands! You can see the design I added and the pot becoming more round to create a vase/lantern esque shape.

Here is my plant holder within my previously made desk organizer! It fit in the slot perfectly and stays very secure. The image at the top of this page gives you a full view of the pot. This was one of my favorite assignments so far. The time put into it was truly worth it and I was entertained the whole way through! :D
