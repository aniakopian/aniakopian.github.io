Week 2 - CNC Routing

Creating a Desk Organizer

CNC Routing! Right above you can see what I created using the shaper in our FabLab! This whole process began with a software system called Fusion 360 which is used for designing and manufacturing 3D models. We used it to design our organizer. I started by inserting the proper measurements for a simple rectangle. Our wood measured at a width of 12.5in and a height of 8.5in.

The one required shape in our organzier was a circle with the diamter of 3in. I started off by creating that first and placing it in my desired location. I had a sketch of a planned organizer, but decided to play around a bit with the software to see what looks best with accurate measurement.

I created some slots to hold my wallet, jewelry, phone, and lip balms. I measured each of those items out to make sure they would fit properly while leaving a space to carve my name.

I wasn't the biggest fan of the sharp edges within the organizer so I decided to filet almost all sharp corners for a better look.

For the 3in circle, we also needed to create another within it that measured at 2.25in. This is so that our cup, which we will be making in the future, would sit right inside the indent and not fall over.

I decided to replace the holes for my lip balm. I thought it looked odd and I could put something more useful there. I went with my airpods, measured them, and created a small rectangle that I also filleted later on.

In this picture you can see me starting to extrude. Our wood is 3/4in thick therefore if I wish to fully cut something out, my depth will be 0.75in and I can enter this under distance. This extrude was for the outer edge so I could have a rounded edge.

I continued this step under each shape that I want to have a pocket-cut. This time, I only chose a depth of 0.50in so that my items could rest well in the pockets and any further would cut straight through the wood.

Here you can see the organizer under render. When you render you can see the model as either its full 3D figure, or as a two-dimensional sketch.

Here is the same render but with an added material feature. I selected wood and a stain that I thought visualized ours best.

I then went and saved the sketch, only the sketch!! I first saved it to my computer and the exported it as a DXF file. From there, I needed to convert the file to be compatable with Adobe Illustrator. I used Convertio to convert the DXF to AI and this was the end of using Fusion 360 for now. I then proceeded to open this file in AI and get the sketch from Fusion 360 to align with the pages measurements. After this, I exported the file to the Shaper's flashdrive which inserts into the CNC router and uploads my design.

For our training, when learning how to use the shaper, these 6 rules had to be checked and followed before doing any work. We started by adding double sided tape to the would and placing it down, once secured we drilled two screws for added security. I also made sure to remove any jewelry on me as well as tie my hair back. Eventually I also had to wear safety glasses and headphones!!

Here is a look at the shaper! I did not get a picture of the shaper from my training session but this is what it looks like before I went in to do my cuts on my organizer. Before being able to do this I needed to scan the area with the machine. To do this you simply drag the shaper across your surface, with the tape still in frame, and once you scan the entire area and get good coverage, you are ready to begin.

When transferring my file to Adobe Illustrator, I had to go in and use a template which shows what colors to use in your design to specify certain cuts. For example,

When transferring my file to Adobe Illustrator, I had to go in and use a template which shows what colors to use in your design to specify certain cuts. For example, a grey cut, signifies a pocket cut, which is what I used for a majority of my organizer.

In this image you can see the two bits we use. The one attached is a 1/4" Up-Spiral Router Bit, and the other is a V Engraving Bit. The 1/4" is used for pocket cuts, or any of the larger cuts. The V bit is great for engraving text or small designs. When you plan on swapping these out you have to ensure they are secured in before beginning any cuts.

Here you can see the swapped out 1/4" for the V-bit before I engraved my name.

This is the shaper before I do an inside cut. I do the inside cut after my pocket cut to clean up the edges. You can see on the top left that the depth is only 0.25", this is so I do not go completely through the wood and you can also only go 0.25" at a time.

The bottom right shows the engraving I completed with the v-bit, and the first pocket cut along with inside cut I did on the top left. The edges were still a bit rough so I planned on doing some sanding towards the end.

Only one more cut left! I continued with the pocket cuts for the rest of my sections. I did a depth of 0.50" for all except the two squares that held my jewelry, those I left at 0.25".

Professor Dunaway and I noticed that if I were to do an exterior cut, it would hit the screws, we went with an interior cut instead and it worked out fine! With this cut I went to 0.75" depth in order to get rid of the border. When doing this I also added four tabs to make sure the wood did not disattach from the surface.

Here is the organizer after extruding the outer border! The tabs were still poking out so I needed to sand those as well.

Time to sand!! I sanded every inch of this organizer to ensure there was no wood sticking out and that it was nice and smooth for the final product!
